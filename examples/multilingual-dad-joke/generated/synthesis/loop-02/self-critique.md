# Self-Critique: synthesis loop 02

## Loop 01 feedback consumed
Loop 01 found that fixing Spanish and Japanese by making adjacency explicit had introduced avoidable explanatory wording in English and French. Its durable lesson was: “When panel feedback exposes a lexical seam, repair the local term without padding every language with explanatory wording; semantic equivalence matters more than literal structural symmetry.”

## Material improvement
- English: `the wall next to it` became the shorter native formula `the other`.
- French: `au mur d’à côté` became `à l’autre`, and the setup became a compact generic riddle.
- Spanish: `a la de al lado` became `a la otra`, while panel-required inner-corner `rincón` was retained.
- Japanese: changed `何て言った` to generic present `何て言う`; retained natural `隣の壁`, no numeric wall counter, and no quotation marks.
- All four setups now use a timeless riddle framing where native usage permits it.

## Completeness gate
PASS. All four required languages and every candidate field are present. Japanese contains kanji and kana (`壁が隣の壁に何て言う？ 角で会おう！`). French and Spanish punctuation/diacritics are present and natural.

## Remaining limitations
- Spanish `rincón` accurately names an inner room corner but has less of the street-corner rendezvous association carried by English `corner` and French `coin`; the in-room phrase is still natural and preserves the literal wall geometry.
- Japanese `角` supplies the geometry cleanly, but the social/physical double reading is a conceptual literalization rather than a lexical pun.
- No independent panel has yet compared this repaired synthesis against C and F.

## Durable lesson
Cross-language equivalence should preserve the same comic inference, not the same word: choosing Spanish `rincón` over a literal-looking `esquina` makes the geometry more exact even though the lexical associations differ.

## Decision
Keep for synthesis. This is the stronger of the two synthesis loops, but it remains pending independent final-panel validation.
