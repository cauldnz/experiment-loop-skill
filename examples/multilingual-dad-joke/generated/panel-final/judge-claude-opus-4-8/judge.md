## Per-candidate notes

### P — eyebrows visual gag (champion)
The mechanism is a purely visual gag — drawn-too-high eyebrows read as a "surprised" face. Because the humor rests on a universally shared facial expression rather than a language-bound pun, the joke translates with zero drift and is equally funny in all four languages. Native scripts present, every rendering idiomatic, JA dialogue natural. Its only minor cost is a slightly longer setup than the wall jokes.

### S — compact revised walls (finalist)
The tightest wall variant. Consistent present tense, canonical EN ("one wall...the other"), and a compact, natural JA line. Uses "rincón" — the geometrically correct interior corner. Defect: "rincón" is precise but not the idiomatic Spanish phrase for people agreeing to meet, softening the double meaning.

### Q — adjacent walls geometry (finalist)
Mechanically equivalent to S with natural FR "au coin" and JA phrasing. Loses a brevity point because the EN "the wall next to it" is wordier than S's "the other". Shares S's rincón tradeoff.

### R — walls original (eliminate)
Its ES "esquina" actually carries the human "let's meet at the corner" connotation better than rincón — a genuine strength. But the JA setup ("一つの壁がもう一つの壁に何て言った？") is literal and clunky versus Q/S, lowering naturalness, and "esquina" is the exterior/street corner, a slight geometric mismatch with walls. Least polished wall variant.

## Panel note
All four pass the completeness gate (all languages + native scripts). **P wins decisively** because a visual-gag mechanism sidesteps the translation risk that the wall jokes carry. **Preserved dissent:** the Spanish punchline forces a rincón-vs-esquina choice — geometric accuracy (Q/S) versus meeting-phrase idiom (R). A judge weighting punchline idiom over geometric precision could plausibly lift R above Q/S. S and Q are tied at 4.2; S is placed higher only on marginally tighter EN/JA phrasing.
