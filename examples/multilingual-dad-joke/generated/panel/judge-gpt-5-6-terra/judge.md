# Blind Panel Vote — J2

**Judge:** `judge-gpt-5-6-terra`  
**Model:** `gpt-5.6-terra`  
**Evidence inspected:** only the six anonymous candidate descriptions and their EN/FR/ES/JA renderings supplied in the prompt. No candidate provenance was considered.

## Method

I first applied the blocking completeness rule. Every candidate supplies English, French, Spanish, and Japanese, and every Japanese entry uses Japanese script. Therefore, all six are eligible. I then scored each of the five equally weighted criteria from 1 to 5 and calculated the arithmetic mean.

The principal distinction was whether the comic mechanism itself travels. Literal semantic preservation is not enough: a translation that retains words while losing a naturally recognizable joke was scored down for equivalence, portability, and naturalness.

## Candidate findings

### A — Eligible, 4.4

The shadow-following observation is shared by all four versions and needs no culture-specific knowledge. It is very concise, but the conclusion is more cute than a pronounced dad-joke groan. The Japanese is clear, though a touch less idiomatic in its phrasing.

### B — Eligible, 2.0

This is an English name-misreading construction. English can treat “Hungry” as a mock name, but *Faim*, *Hambre*, and お腹すいた do not naturally invite the same response. The non-English texts are understandable literal recreations, not equivalent jokes. Japanese is especially forced.

### C — Eligible, 4.8

This is the winner. Two walls meeting at a corner is a universal spatial fact, and “meet at the corner” provides the same compact punch line in each language. The Japanese question is slightly stiff in its “one wall/another wall” wording, but the joke remains natural and immediate.

### D — Eligible, 4.6

An echo repeating secrets is a strong, language-independent personification gag. All four renderings preserve the setup and payoff. The French expression for keeping secrets and the Spanish object phrasing are mildly less idiomatic than ideal, but neither compromises comprehension or the comic effect.

### E — Eligible, 2.0

This has the same cross-language flaw as B. The English pattern can make “Tired” sound like a name, while the French, Spanish, and Japanese versions instead read as an unnatural insistence on naming a state. It is short, but the translated comic effect does not survive.

### F — Eligible, 4.6

The joke is visual rather than dependent on a single language’s homophony or syntax: high-drawn eyebrows make a surprised face. That visual inference works in all four languages and produces a reliably groan-worthy dad-joke payoff. It is slightly longer than the best question-and-answer entries, and the French and Spanish are marginally less pun-tight than English.

## Final ranking

1. **C** — 4.8
2. **F** — 4.6
3. **D** — 4.6
4. **A** — 4.4
5. **B** — 2.0
6. **E** — 2.0

F is ranked above D at the equal mean because its visual mechanism is particularly robust across languages; D has small but real phrasing roughness in both French and Spanish. B and E are retained in the ranking because they pass the objective completeness gate, not because they are viable multilingual finalists.
